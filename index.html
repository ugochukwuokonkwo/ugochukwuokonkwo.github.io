Master's Thesis: Ergonomic Redesign of an Assistive Button Hook for Individuals with Dexterity Impairments

Graduate research project focused on redesigning a traditional assistive button hook to improve comfort, usability, and functional performance for individuals experiencing dexterity impairments. The project utilized a human-centered design process involving clinician collaboration, ergonomic evaluation, concept development, rapid prototyping, fixture design, and usability testing.

  • Conducted needs assessment activities with physical therapy faculty and clinicians to identify limitations of existing assistive dressing devices and establish user-centered design requirements.
  • Performed ergonomic benchmarking and handle evaluations to identify design opportunities for improving comfort, grip, orientation, and ease of use.
  • Generated and evaluated multiple mechanism concepts including picker-style, scroll-assisted, cross-linked forceps, and two-finger pinch approaches.
  • Developed and refined a forceps-inspired wire mechanism designed to improve thread manipulation and button engagement while minimizing dexterity requirements.
  • Created multiple CAD models and ergonomic handle concepts to support iterative design development and user-centered refinement.
  • Fabricated and evaluated numerous 3D printed handle iterations to optimize comfort, usability, and device orientation.
  • Designed and fabricated custom wire-forming jigs using CAD and CO₂ laser cutting to accurately manufacture spring-wire mechanism components.
  • Developed and tested two alternative mechanism configurations to evaluate usability, actuation requirements, and thread-capture performance.
  • Conducted usability testing and user evaluations to assess effectiveness, comfort, ease of use, and overall user satisfaction.
  • Applied principles of human-centered design, ergonomics, rapid prototyping, usability engineering, and medical device product development throughout the project lifecycle.

Low-Cost Versatile Electrospinning System

Undergraduate Senior Design Project focused on the design, fabrication, integration, and validation of a low-cost electrospinning platform capable of producing both aligned and random polymeric nanofibers for tissue engineering applications.

  • Mapped out the overall system architecture and integrated the syringe pump, high-voltage power supply, rotating collector, enclosure, and control systems into a unified electrospinning platform.
  • Designed the system enclosure and structural layout using CAD, creating a modular aluminum-frame and acrylic-panel system for safe operation and maintenance accessibility.
  • Fabricated and assembled the enclosure using aluminum railings, acrylic panels, fastening systems, tapping operations, and fabrication tools.
  • Integrated mechanical, electrical, and motion-control subsystems including the syringe pump, NEMA17 stepper motor, rotating drum collector, motor holder, and control electronics.
  • Designed and 3D printed custom system components including the drum collector, collector stand, syringe base, syringe motor holder, and syringe pusher mechanism.
  • Conducted testing and parameter optimization involving flow rate, collector speed, and polymer delivery conditions to improve nanofiber consistency and alignment.
  • Implemented safety-focused design features including enclosure protection, grounding considerations, and high-voltage isolation measures.

Computational Bioheat Modeling of Spinal Cord Thermal Distribution

Graduate research project using ANSYS Fluent to investigate heat transfer through spinal tissues and evaluate whether controlled surface heating could reproduce therapeutic thermal effects associated with electrical stimulation-based pain modulation.

  • Developed a multilayer anatomical spinal model consisting of spinal cord, cerebrospinal fluid (CSF), epidural fat, dura/bone, and skin-fat-muscle tissue layers.
  • Implemented transient thermal simulations in ANSYS Fluent using literature-derived biological tissue thermal properties.
  • Evaluated heat conduction through spinal tissues under a 45°C external heating boundary condition.
  • Performed mesh independence studies using 0.4 mm, 0.2 mm, and 0.1 mm mesh densities to improve numerical accuracy and computational efficiency.
  • Analyzed time-dependent spinal cord temperature response over a 30-minute heating period.
  • Determined that a therapeutic spinal cord temperature increase of approximately 1.78°C could be achieved after about 8 minutes of controlled heating.
  • Demonstrated the potential for non-invasive surface heating to reproduce central thermal effects comparable to electrical stimulation approaches.

Protein Analysis of Regular vs. Vegan Chicken Under Different Cooking Methods

Biomedical engineering laboratory research project investigating the effect of different cooking methods on protein concentration in traditional and plant-based chicken products using protein extraction and Bradford Assay quantification techniques.

  • Designed and executed an experimental study comparing protein concentration in regular and vegan chicken samples.
  • Prepared raw, boiled, grilled, pan-fried, and air-fried samples for comparative analysis.
  • Performed protein extraction using RIPA buffer protocols and serial dilution techniques.
  • Conducted Bradford Assays using BSA standards and microplate analysis to quantify protein concentration.
  • Generated calibration curves and analyzed absorbance measurements to determine protein content.
  • Evaluated the influence of cooking methods on protein retention and concentration.
  • Documented experimental limitations including sample preparation differences and potential cross-contamination sources.
  • Presented findings through technical reports, graphical data analysis, and scientific presentations.

Key Findings: Vegan chicken exhibited higher measured protein concentrations than regular chicken across most cooking conditions, with the exception of raw samples.
